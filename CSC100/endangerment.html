Threats

Red pandas' main threats are habitat loss due to climate change, natural disaster and human development as well as poaching. Bamboo is in great demand because the natural growth is severely harvested taking away a vital food supply from the red pandas. Red panda pelts are also poached and sold on the black market for a considerable sum. Even with all the laws and conservation efforts in place the red panda's habitat is being significantly demolished. As humans move into red panda habitat, domestic dogs are becoming a new predator to the pandas. The current low population is causing inbreeding within the species decreasing new cub's survival rates.

Conservation

Although, China, Nepal, India, Bhutan and Myanmar all have laws to protect their red panda populations the species population continues to decline. The red panda population has declined by 40% since 2000 and experts believe their numbers will continue to decrease. Zoos have been amazing supports in preserving the red panda population by maintaining self-sustaining populations. Red panda's unique habitat makes conservation quite difficult as the animals require a specific environment for survival. The lack of bamboo and sufficient nutrients is one of the most challenging aspects of conservation. Three organizations that have put in tremendous effort for conserving the red panda population are the Smithsonian's National Zoo and Conservation Biology Institute, the International Union for Conservation of Nature, and the World Wildlife Fund. The main efforts have been to improve awareness, reduce habitat degradation, protect against habitat loss, and ultimately reduce deaths of red pandas. Observation of red pandas is quite difficult because of the animals secluded nature.

The Panchthar-Ilam-Taplejung Red Panda Protected Forest in Nepal is home to the densest population of Red Pandas. The forest is alive with many endangered species and has strong biodiversity.
